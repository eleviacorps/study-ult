## 1.1 Introduction to Electrostatics

### What is Electrostatics?
Electrostatics is the study of charges at rest and the forces, fields, and potentials they produce.

### Real-World Manifestations
1. **Spark/ckling when removing synthetic clothes** - Due to static electricity buildup and discharge
2. **Lightning during thunderstorms** - Massive electrostatic discharge between clouds and ground
3. **Electric shock from car door/iron bar** - Discharge of accumulated charge through body

> [!KEY-CONCEPT]
> **Static electricity** refers to charges that don't move (compared to current electricity where charges flow). The "static" doesn't mean the charges can't move — it means the configuration of charges is not changing with time in the system being studied.

## 1.2 Discovery of Electric Charge

### Historical Context
- **Thales of Miletus (600 BC)**: First to observe that amber rubbed with wool attracts light objects
- The word "electricity" comes from Greek word **elektron** meaning **amber**

### Fundamental Observations (Figure 1.1)

| Experiment | Observation | Conclusion |
|------------|-------------|------------|
| Two glass rods (rubbed with silk) brought together | Repel each other | Same charge |
| Two silk cloths brought together | Repel each other | Same charge |
| Glass rod + silk cloth | Attract each other | Opposite charges |
| Two plastic rods (rubbed with fur) | Repel each other | Same charge |
| Plastic rod + fur | Attract each other | Opposite charges |
| Glass rod + plastic rod | Attract each other | Opposite charges |

### The Two-Charge System
1. **Like charges repel** - Two positive charges push apart; two negative charges push apart
2. **Unlike charges attract** - Positive and negative charges pull toward each other

> [!DEFINITION]
> **Polarity** is the property that differentiates the two types of charges. By convention:
> - Charge on glass rod (rubbed with silk) = **Positive (+)**  
> - Charge on plastic rod (rubbed with fur) = **Negative (−)**

## 1.3 How Objects Get Charged

### Charging by Rubbing (Triboelectric Charging)
When you rub two objects together:
- Electrons transfer from one material to another
- The material losing electrons becomes **positively charged**
- The material gaining electrons becomes **negatively charged**

### Example: Glass Rod + Silk
- Electrons move **from glass to silk**
- Glass rod → positive charge
- Silk cloth → negative charge

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **No new charge is created or destroyed** — only charge transfers between bodies. This is consistent with the **law of conservation of charge**.

### How Much Charge Transfers?
- The number of electrons transferred is a **very small fraction** of total electrons in the material
- Even a small static shock involves billions of electrons

## 1.4 Conduction vs. Insulation

### Conductors
Materials that allow electric charge to flow through them easily.

**Examples:**
- Metals (copper, aluminum, gold)
- Human bodies
- Earth (ground)

**Property:** Charges are **free to move** inside the material.

### Insulators
Materials that **do not allow** electric charge to flow through them easily.

**Examples:**
- Glass, porcelain, plastic
- Nylon, rubber, wood

**Property:** Charges **stay at the location** where they are placed.

### Semiconductors
Materials with resistance **between** conductors and insulators. Used in electronic devices.

> [!PRACTICAL-APPLICATION]
> **Why plastic comb gets charged but metal spoon doesn't:**
> - Plastic comb: acts as insulator → charge stays
> - Metal spoon: acts as conductor → charge leaks through your body to ground
> - Exception: If you hold a metal spoon by its plastic handle, it can hold charge

## 1.5 Basic Properties of Electric Charge

### Property 1: Additivity of Charges

**Definition:** The total charge of a system is the **algebraic sum** of all individual charges.

$$q_{total} = q_1 + q_2 + q_3 + \ldots + q_n$$

**Key Points:**
- Charges add like real numbers (scalar addition)
- Can be positive or negative
- Unlike mass (always positive), charge can have signs

**Example:**
System with charges: +1, +2, -3, +4, -5 units
$$q_{total} = (+1) + (+2) + (-3) + (+4) + (-5) = -1 \text{ unit}$$

> [!TIP]
> **Remember:** When adding charges, always include the sign! A +5 and -3 give +2, not 8.

---

### Property 2: Conservation of Charge

**Law:** In an isolated system, the **total charge remains constant** — no charge is created or destroyed.

**Applications:**
- When bodies are charged by rubbing: charge transfers, doesn't appear out of nowhere
- When particles are created (e.g., neutron → proton + electron): total charge before = total charge after = 0

**Verification:**
- Charge conservation has been experimentally verified to extremely high precision
- Applies to all known physical processes

> [!IMPORTANT]
> A neutron (charge = 0) decays into a proton (+e) and electron (-e). Total charge before = 0, after = (+e) + (-e) = 0. Conservation maintained!

---

### Property 3: Quantisation of Charge

**Law:** All free charges are **integral multiples** of a basic unit of charge 'e'.

$$q = n \cdot e$$

Where:
- $e = 1.602192 \times 10^{-19}$ C (elementary charge)
- $n = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \pm3, \ldots$ (integer)

**Physical Meaning:**
- Charge on electron = $-e$ (negative)
- Charge on proton = $+e$ (positive)
- Any observable charge must be a whole number multiple of 'e'

> [!KEY-CONCEPT]
> The **quantisation of charge** was first suggested by Faraday's laws of electrolysis and experimentally demonstrated by Millikan in 1912 (Oil Drop Experiment).

## 1.6 Important Numbers to Remember

| Quantity | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Elementary charge (e) | $1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C |
| Electrons per coulomb | $\approx 6.25 \times 10^{18}$ |
| Coulomb's constant (k) | $9 \times 10^9$ N·m²/C² |
| Permittivity of free space (ε₀) | $8.854 \times 10^{-12}$ C²/N·m² |

### SI Unit of Charge: Coulomb (C)
Defined as: The charge that flows through a wire in 1 second when current is 1 ampere.

$$1 \text{ C} = 1 \text{ A} \times 1 \text{ s}$$

### Practical Sub-units
| Unit | Symbol | Conversion |
|------|--------|------------|
| Microcoulomb | μC | $10^{-6}$ C |
| Nanocoulomb | nC | $10^{-9}$ C |
| Picocoulomb | pC | $10^{-12}$ C |

## 1.7 Solved Examples

### Example 1: Electrons to form 1 Coulomb
**Problem:** If $10^9$ electrons move out of a body every second, how long to accumulate 1 C?

**Solution:**
Charge per second = $10^9 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C = $1.6 \times 10^{-10}$ C/s

Time required = $\frac{1 \text{ C}}{1.6 \times 10^{-10} \text{ C/s}} = 6.25 \times 10^9$ s

In years = $\frac{6.25 \times 10^9}{365 \times 24 \times 3600} \approx 198$ years

> [!INSIGHT]
> 1 Coulomb is a **very large unit** for static electricity purposes. This is why we use μC and nC in electrostatics.

---

### Example 2: Charge in a cup of water
**Problem:** Calculate total positive and negative charge in 250g of water.

**Solution:**
- Moles of water = $\frac{250}{18} = 13.89$ mol
- Molecules = $13.89 \times 6.02 \times 10^{23} = 8.36 \times 10^{24}$ molecules
- Each molecule: 10 electrons + 10 protons
- Total charge magnitude = $8.36 \times 10^{24} \times 10 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C
- = $1.34 \times 10^7$ C (both + and −)

> [!MIND-BLOW]
> A glass of water contains approximately **13 million Coulombs** of positive and negative charge — but they cancel perfectly, making the net charge zero!

## 1.8 Charge Detection - Gold Leaf Electroscope

### Construction
1. Vertical metal rod housed in a box
2. Two thin gold leaves attached at bottom
3. Metal knob at top for charging

### Working Principle
1. When charged object touches the knob, charge flows onto leaves
2. Both leaves acquire same charge → they repel each other
3. Divergence indicates charge magnitude

### Limitation
The electroscope shows charge presence but cannot measure exact quantity precisely.

## 1.9 Why Do Objects Have Charge?

### Atomic Perspective
All matter consists of atoms containing:
- **Protons** in nucleus → positive charge
- **Electrons** orbiting nucleus → negative charge
- **Neutrons** in nucleus → no charge

### Normally Neutral State
In normal conditions, number of protons = number of electrons
→ Net charge = 0

### Becoming Charged
- **Positive ion:** Loss of electrons (more protons than electrons)
- **Negative ion:** Gain of electrons (more electrons than protons)

> [!FUNDAMENTAL]
> In solids, electrons are the mobile charges that transfer during charging. In electrolytes, both positive and negative ions can be mobile.

## Common Student Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Confusing charge transfer direction
**Wrong:** Thinking electrons move from silk to glass when glass is rubbed with silk
**Correct:** Electrons transfer from glass to silk (glass loses electrons, becomes +)

**Trick:** Remember: "When glass is rubbed, it LOSES electrons" — "GLASS" starts with G, "GIVES" electrons.

### Mistake 2: Forgetting signs in charge addition
**Wrong:** $+5\mu C + (-3\mu C) = 8\mu C$
**Correct:** $+5\mu C + (-3\mu C) = +2\mu C$

### Mistake 3: Thinking 1C is a small amount
**Reality:** 1C is enormous for electrostatics. Most experiments involve nC to μC range.
