# Electrostatics Core Map

Electrostatics studies charges at rest and the electric fields created by them. The chapter links [[Coulomb Law]], [[Electric Field]], [[Gauss Law]], [[Electric Potential]], and [[Capacitance]] into one graph of ideas.

> [!idea]
> Think of electric field as the local instruction a positive test charge would follow.

## High Yield Spine

- Charge is quantized and conserved.
- Field lines begin on positive charge and end on negative charge.
- Symmetry decides whether [[Gauss Law]] is useful.
- Potential is scalar and often easier than vector field addition.

## Formula Anchors

Point-charge force:

$$F = k\frac{q_1q_2}{r^2}$$

Electric field due to a point charge:

$$E = k\frac{q}{r^2}$$

- Q:: When should Gauss Law be preferred over direct integration? A:: Use it when the charge distribution has spherical, cylindrical, or planar symmetry so the field is constant over a Gaussian surface.
- Q:: Why is electric potential easier in many multi-charge problems? A:: Potential is scalar, so contributions add algebraically instead of vectorially.
- MCQ:: The electric field inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is: Options:: Zero | Constant non-zero | Infinite | Direction dependent Answer:: Zero
