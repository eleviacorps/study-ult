## 5.1 Concept of Field Lines

### Why Field Lines?
- Electric field is a **vector field** (has magnitude and direction at each point)
- Visualizing 3D vector fields is difficult
- Field lines provide a **pictorial representation** of field direction and relative strength

### Definition
An electric field line is a curve drawn such that the **tangent at any point** gives the direction of the electric field at that point.

> [!KEY-CONCEPT]
> Field lines are a **visualization tool** invented by Faraday (who called them "lines of force"). They don't have physical existence but help us understand field patterns.

---

## 5.2 Drawing Field Lines

### Procedure
1. At each point of space, draw a small arrow in direction of E
2. Connect arrows pointing in similar directions to form continuous curves
3. Length/density of arrows indicates field strength

### Key Rule
**Tangent to field line at any point = direction of E at that point**

---

## 5.3 Density of Field Lines = Field Strength

### Rule
- **More dense field lines** = Stronger electric field
- **Less dense field lines** = Weaker electric field

### Why Density Matters
Number of field lines crossing a unit area perpendicular to field direction is proportional to |E|.

**For point charge:**
- At distance r: Field lines spread over spherical surface of area $4\pi r^2$
- As r increases, area increases → lines spread out → density decreases
- Consistent with $E \propto 1/r^2$

---

## 5.4 Rules of Electric Field Lines

### Rule 1: Origin and Termination
- Field lines **start from positive charges** and **end at negative charges**
- In a charge-free region, lines can continue through space
- For single isolated charge, lines go to infinity (or come from infinity)

### Rule 2: Continuity
Field lines are **continuous curves** with no sudden breaks.

**Why no breaks?**
If a line started and ended in same region without charge, we'd have contradictory directions at break point.

### Rule 3: No Crossing
**Two field lines can NEVER cross each other.**

**Reason:**
At point of intersection, the tangent would have two different directions → field would have two different directions simultaneously. Impossible!

### Rule 4: No Closed Loops
Electrostatic field lines **never form closed loops**.

**Reason:**
Electric field is conservative (path independent). Closed loop would imply work done in cycle ≠ 0, contradicting conservative nature.

## 5.5 Field Line Patterns

### Pattern 1: Single Positive Point Charge
- Lines radiate **outward** from charge
- Radial symmetry
- Density decreases with distance
- Spacing: closer near charge, wider apart far away

### Pattern 2: Single Negative Point Charge
- Lines radiate **inward** toward charge
- Radial symmetry
- Same density pattern as positive charge

### Pattern 3: Two Equal Positive Charges
- Lines repel each other
- Repulsion pattern visible in middle region
- No field lines in the exact middle (neutral point)
- Field strong in region between charges

### Pattern 4: Electric Dipole (+q and -q)
- Lines start at +q, end at -q
- Form curved paths connecting the charges
- Dense in region between charges (attraction)
- Show characteristic "teardrop" pattern

> [!VISUAL-INSIGHT]
> Field line patterns visually show:
> - **Repulsion**: Lines pushing apart
> - **Attraction**: Lines converging between opposite charges
> - **Neutral points**: Regions where lines neither start nor end

## 5.6 Tangent and Normal to Field Lines

### At Any Point on Field Line:
- **Tangent direction** = Direction of electric field (E)
- **Normal direction** = Direction of maximum rate of change of field

### Understanding Tangent
If you place a positive test charge on a field line and let it move (neglecting inertia), it will follow the field line path.

---

## 5.7 Mathematical Connection

### Solid Angle Concept
For a point charge:
- Number of field lines in solid angle $\Delta\Omega$ is constant
- Area element at distance r: $\Delta S = r^2 \Delta\Omega$
- Field lines per unit area: $n/r^2$ where n is total lines
- Since $E \propto 1/r^2$, field strength is proportional to line density

### Flux through Surface
The number of field lines crossing a surface is related to **electric flux**:
$$\phi = \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{A} = EA\cos\theta$$

## 5.8 Types of Electric Fields by Line Patterns

### Uniform Field
- Field lines: Parallel, equally spaced
- E has same magnitude and direction everywhere
- Example: Between two infinite parallel charged plates

### Radial Field
- Field lines: Radial from center
- E decreases with distance (1/r²)
- Example: Near point charge

### Dipole Field
- Field lines: Connect from + to - through curved paths
- More complex pattern than radial field
- Net dipole moment determines field at large distances

## 5.9 Important Properties Summary

| Property | Description |

| Start point | Positive charge (or infinity) |
| End point | Negative charge (or infinity) |
| Tangent direction | Direction of E |
| Line density | Proportional to \|E\| |
| Line spacing | Closer = stronger field |
| Intersection | Never cross |
| Closed loops | Never form closed loops |

## 5.10 Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: Field lines have fixed length
**Reality:** We can draw as many lines as we want - it's relative density that matters, not absolute number.

### Misconception 2: Field lines are paths of moving charges
**Reality:** Field lines show direction of force, not necessarily the path a charge would follow (unless specific conditions).

### Misconception 3: Two lines crossing at different planes
**Clarification:** Lines can cross in 3D projection even if they don't intersect in space. In proper 3D representation, they still don't cross.

## 5.11 Quick Check

**Q: Can field lines be curved?**
Yes! Field lines around a dipole are curved. Tangent at any point gives E direction.

**Q: Where is field strongest?**
Where field lines are most densely packed.

**Q: What happens to field lines inside a uniformly charged spherical shell?**
They terminate on the surface - zero field inside means no lines inside.

## 5.12 JEE Questions on Field Lines

### Common Question Type
Show field line diagram and ask about:
- Charge signs
- Relative magnitudes
- Regions of strongest/weakest field
- Correctness of diagram

### Example
**Q:** Which diagram correctly shows field lines for two equal positive charges?
**Key:** Lines should diverge from both charges, never cross, be symmetric.
