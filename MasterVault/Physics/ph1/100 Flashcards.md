### SECTION 1: Basic Charge Properties (1-20)

**Q1:** What does quantisation of charge mean?
**A:** Electric charge exists only as integer multiples of elementary charge e: q = ne, where n = 0, ±1, ±2, ±3...

---

**Q2:** State the three fundamental properties of electric charge.
**A:** 1. Quantisation: q = ne, 2. Additivity: charges add algebraically, 3. Conservation: total charge of isolated system remains constant.

---

**Q3:** What is the value of elementary charge e?
**A:** e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (on both electron and proton, with sign: electron = -e, proton = +e)

---

**Q4:** How many electrons constitute 1 coulomb of charge?
**A:** n = Q/e = 1/(1.6×10⁻¹⁹) ≈ 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons

---

**Q5:** State the law of conservation of charge.
**A:** Total charge of an isolated system remains constant. Charge can neither be created nor destroyed, but can be transferred.

---

**Q6:** What happens when a glass rod is rubbed with silk?
**A:** Electrons transfer from glass to silk. Glass loses electrons → positive charge. Silk gains electrons → negative charge.

---

**Q7:** Why does plastic get charged on rubbing but metal doesn't (when held in hand)?
**A:** Plastic is an insulator → charge stays. Metal is a conductor → charge leaks through body to ground.

---

**Q8:** What is the difference between conductors and insulators?
**A:** Conductors: charges free to move; Insulators: charges bound in place. Examples: Metals/Cu/Ag are conductors; Glass/Plastic/Rubber are insulators.

---

**Q9:** Why do like charges repel and unlike charges attract?
**A:** This is a fundamental experimental observation. Coulomb's law with positive constant k gives repulsion for same-sign charges and attraction for opposite-sign charges.

---

**Q10:** What is the SI unit of electric charge?
**A:** Coulomb (C). Defined as: charge flowing through wire in 1 second when current is 1 ampere (1 C = 1 A × 1 s)

---

**Q11:** What are the practical sub-units of coulomb?
**A:** 1 μC (microcoulomb) = 10⁻⁶ C; 1 nC (nanocoulomb) = 10⁻⁹ C; 1 pC (picocoulomb) = 10⁻¹² C

---

**Q12:** Why is quantisation of charge not observable at macroscopic scale?
**A:** Because elementary charge e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C is extremely small. Macroscopic charges involve ~10¹³ times this value, so discrete steps are undetectable.

---

**Q13:** What is triboelectric charging?
**A:** Charging by rubbing two bodies together, causing electron transfer. The triboelectric series lists materials in order of tendency to gain/lose electrons.

---

**Q14:** Define additivity of charge.
**A:** Total charge of a system = algebraic sum of individual charges. Like mass, charge is a scalar quantity.

---

**Q15:** Can charge exist without mass?
**A:** Yes, photons (particles of light) carry energy and momentum but have zero charge. But in everyday physics, charges are electrons/protons which have mass.

---

**Q16:** State whether charge is scalar or vector.
**A:** Charge is a scalar quantity (has magnitude, no direction). However, forces between charges are vectors.

---

**Q17:** What is the charge on an alpha particle?
**A:** Alpha particle = helium nucleus = 2 protons + 2 neutrons. Charge = +2e = +3.2×10⁻¹⁹ C

---

**Q18:** If a body has 5×10¹⁸ electrons in excess, what is its charge?
**A:** Q = ne = 5×10¹⁸ × (-1.6×10⁻¹⁹) = -0.8 C (negative charge)

---

**Q19:** What is the charge on a neutron?
**A:** Zero. Neutron has no net charge (composed of quarks that sum to zero charge).

---

**Q20:** How is charging by conduction different from charging by induction?
**A:** Conduction: physical contact between bodies; charge spreads. Induction: charged body brought near (no contact), causes redistribution, then grounding removes charge.

---

### SECTION 2: Coulomb's Law (21-35)

**Q21:** State Coulomb's law in words.
**A:** Force between two point charges is directly proportional to product of their charges and inversely proportional to square of distance between them, acting along the line joining the charges.

---

**Q22:** Write Coulomb's law formula.
**A:** F = k × (q₁q₂)/r² = (1/4πε₀) × (q₁q₂)/r²

---

**Q23:** What is the value of Coulomb's constant k?
**A:** k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C² = 1/(4πε₀)

---

**Q24:** What is permittivity of free space ε₀?
**A:** ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²/N·m²

---

**Q25:** Coulomb's law F = kq₁q₂/r². What happens if q₁ and q₂ have same sign?
**A:** Force is positive (repulsive). If opposite signs, force is negative (attractive).

---

**Q26:** Compare magnitude of Coulomb force and gravitational force between two protons.
**A:** Fₑ/Fɢ ≈ 1.3 × 10³⁶. Electric force is ~10³⁶ times stronger than gravitational force between protons.

---

**Q27:** Why is gravitational force negligible compared to electric force at atomic scale?
**A:** Electric forces involve charges where both attraction and repulsion occur, allowing cancellation. Gravity only attracts. At atomic scale, Fₑ/Fɢ ~ 10³⁹.

---

**Q28:** If distance between charges is halved, by what factor does force change?
**A:** F ∝ 1/r². Halving r → F increases by factor of 4.

---

**Q29:** Write Coulomb's law in vector form.
**A:** F₂₁ = (kq₁q₂/r₂₁²) × r̂₂₁ (force on 2 due to 1)

---

**Q30:** What is the range of validity of Coulomb's law?
**A:** Tested from ~10⁻¹⁰ m (atomic scale) to ~10⁷ m. Follows inverse-square law extremely accurately (exponent is exactly 2 to 1 part in 10¹⁶).

---

**Q31:** How does Coulomb force change if charges are placed in a medium of dielectric constant K?
**A:** F_new = F_vacuum / K. Force decreases by factor equal to dielectric constant.

---

**Q32:** State one limitation of Coulomb's law.
**A:** Only strictly valid for point charges at rest. For moving charges, magnetic effects appear.

---

**Q33:** If each of two charges is doubled, how does force change?
**A:** F ∝ q₁q₂. Doubling both → F increases by factor of 4.

---

**Q34:** What is the dimensional formula for Coulomb's constant k?
**A:** [ML³T⁻⁴A⁻²] (from F = kq₁q₂/r²)

---

**Q35:** Why is Coulomb's law called an inverse-square law?
**A:** Because force is inversely proportional to the square of distance: F ∝ 1/r²

---

### SECTION 3: Electric Field (36-50)

**Q36:** Define electric field intensity at a point.
**A:** Electric field at a point = force per unit positive test charge placed at that point: E = F/q = limit(q→0) of F/q

---

**Q37:** Write the SI unit of electric field.
**A:** N/C (Newton per Coulomb) = V/m (Volt per meter). Both are equivalent.

---

**Q38:** Electric field due to a point charge Q at distance r is...
**A:** E = kQ/r² (radially outward if Q > 0, inward if Q < 0)

---

**Q39:** What is the direction of electric field for a positive point charge?
**A:** Radially outward (away from the charge).

---

**Q40:** What is the direction of electric field for a negative point charge?
**A:** Radially inward (toward the charge).

---

**Q41:** Force on charge q in electric field E is given by...
**A:** F = qE. Direction: same as E if q > 0, opposite to E if q < 0.

---

**Q42:** State superposition principle for electric fields.
**A:** Total electric field at a point = vector sum of fields due to all individual charges.

---

**Q43:** Electric field inside a uniformly charged spherical shell is...
**A:** Zero (E = 0 everywhere inside).

---

**Q44:** Electric field outside a uniformly charged spherical shell is...
**A:** E = kQ/r², same as if all charge concentrated at center.

---

**Q45:** How does electric field vary with distance for an infinite line charge?
**A:** E ∝ 1/r. E = λ/(2πε₀r) for infinite line.

---

**Q46:** Electric field due to infinite plane sheet is...
**A:** E = σ/(2ε₀), independent of distance from sheet.

---

**Q47:** If electron and proton are placed in same electric field, which experiences greater force?
**A:** Same magnitude force: F = eE for both (but electron accelerates more due to smaller mass).

---

**Q48:** An electron is projected parallel to uniform electric field. What happens?
**A:** It decelerates, stops, then accelerates back (opposite to field direction because charge is negative).

---

**Q49:** Electric field is a scalar/vector quantity?
**A:** Vector quantity (has both magnitude and direction).

---

**Q50:** At a point where electric field is zero, what is potential?
**A:** Potential can be any value (not necessarily zero). E = -dV/dr = 0 means V is constant, not necessarily zero.

---

### SECTION 4: Electric Field Lines (51-60)

**Q51:** Define an electric field line.
**A:** A curve drawn such that tangent at any point gives direction of electric field at that point.

---

**Q52:** State two properties of electric field lines.
**A:** 1. Field lines start from positive charges and end at negative charges. 2. No two field lines can cross each other.

---

**Q53:** Electric field is stronger where field lines are...
**A:** More dense (closer together).

---

**Q54:** Can electric field lines form closed loops in electrostatics?
**A:** No. Electrostatic field lines cannot form closed loops because they start at + charges and end at - charges (or infinity).

---

**Q55:** What do field lines look like for a dipole (+q and -q)?
**A:** Curved lines connecting from +q to -q. Dense in region between charges showing attraction.

---

**Q56:** Field lines for two equal positive charges show...
**A:** Repulsion pattern - lines push away from each other. No lines exist in exact midpoint.

---

**Q57:** Are field lines real physical entities?
**A:** No, they're a visualization tool introduced by Faraday to understand fields. They don't physically exist.

---

**Q58:** What happens to field lines when they are perpendicular to a surface?
**A:** They are normal (perpendicular) to the surface.

---

**Q59:** For a uniform electric field, how are field lines drawn?
**A:** Parallel, equally spaced straight lines.

---

**Q60:** Why don't field lines intersect?
**A:** At point of intersection, field would have two different directions simultaneously, which is physically impossible.

---

### SECTION 5: Electric Flux & Gauss's Law (61-75)

**Q61:** Define electric flux through a surface.
**A:** Φ = E·S = ES cosθ, where θ is angle between E and normal to surface.

---

**Q62:** SI unit of electric flux.
**A:** N·m²/C = V·m

---

**Q63:** State Gauss's law.
**A:** Total electric flux through any closed surface = net charge enclosed/ε₀

---

**Q64:** Write Gauss's law mathematically.
**A:** ∮ E·dS = q_enclosed/ε₀

---

**Q65:** Does Gauss's law depend on shape of Gaussian surface?
**A:** No. Net flux depends only on enclosed charge, not on shape of surface.

---

**Q66:** For flux calculation, what is direction of area vector for closed surface?
**A:** Outward normal (pointing away from interior).

---

**Q67:** Flux through closed surface with no charge inside is...
**A:** Zero.

---

**Q68:** Why is Gauss's law useful?
**A:** It simplifies electric field calculations for symmetric charge distributions by converting surface integral to simple algebra.

---

**Q69:** For a point charge q at center of sphere, flux through sphere is...
**A:** Φ = q/ε₀ (independent of radius of sphere).

---

**Q70:** Can Gaussian surface pass through a point charge?
**A:** No. Field is undefined (infinite) at location of point charge. Gaussian surface can pass through continuous charge distributions.

---

**Q71:** What is electric flux? Scalar or vector?
**A:** Scalar quantity (dot product of two vectors).

---

**Q72:** Flux through one face of cube with charge q at center...
**A:** q/(6ε₀)

---

**Q73:** Two charges +q and -q inside a closed surface with net charge zero. Flux is...
**A:** Zero (since q_enclosed = +q + (-q) = 0)

---

**Q74:** Gauss's law is derived from which law?
**A:** Coulomb's law (with inverse-square dependence).

---

**Q75:** If electric flux through closed surface is Φ, what does Φ > 0 indicate?
**A:** Net positive charge enclosed (more lines leaving than entering).

---

### SECTION 6: Electric Dipole (76-90)

**Q76:** Define electric dipole moment.
**A:** p = q × 2a (vector from negative to positive charge). SI unit: C·m.

---

**Q77:** Electric field on axis of dipole at distance r (r >> a)...
**A:** E_axial = 2p/(4πε₀r³) = 2k p/r³, direction along dipole moment.

---

**Q78:** Electric field on equatorial plane of dipole at distance r...
**A:** E_equatorial = p/(4πε₀r³) = kp/r³, direction opposite to dipole moment.

---

**Q79:** Ratio of field on axis to that on equatorial plane of dipole at same distance...
**A:** 2:1 (axis field is twice equatorial field).

---

**Q80:** Torque on dipole in uniform field E...
**A:** τ = pE sinθ = |p × E|. Maximum when θ = 90°.

---

**Q81:** Potential energy of dipole in uniform field...
**A:** U = -pE cosθ = -p·E. Minimum (-pE) when aligned (θ = 0°), maximum (+pE) when anti-aligned.

---

**Q82:** Net force on dipole in uniform electric field...
**A:** Zero (forces on +q and -q are equal and opposite).

---

**Q83:** Does a neutral molecule have dipole moment always zero?
**A:** Not always. Molecules like H₂O have permanent dipole moment due to asymmetric charge distribution.

---

**Q84:** Give example of polar and non-polar molecules.
**A:** Polar: H₂O, HCl (permanent dipole). Non-polar: CO₂, CH₄ (zero dipole).

---

**Q85:** Why is CO₂ non-polar despite having polar bonds?
**A:** CO₂ is linear (O=C=O). Bond dipoles are equal and opposite, cancel out completely.

---

**Q86:** Dipole field varies as... (dependence on distance)
**A:** E ∝ 1/r³ (for r >> a). Falls off faster than point charge field (1/r²).

---

**Q87:** Work done in rotating dipole from θ₁ to θ₂ in uniform field...
**A:** W = pE(cosθ₁ - cosθ₂)

---

**Q88:** What is stable equilibrium position for dipole in field?
**A:** When p is parallel to E (θ = 0°). Minimum potential energy.

---

**Q89:** Angular frequency of oscillation of dipole in uniform field...
**A:** ω = √(pE/I), where I is moment of inertia about perpendicular axis through center.

---

**Q90:** Does a charge experience force in non-uniform field when placed as part of dipole?
**A:** Yes. In non-uniform field, forces on +q and -q are unequal, resulting in net force AND torque.

---

### SECTION 7: Advanced & Quick Recall (91-100)

**Q91:** Is electric potential energy defined at a point or for a system?
**A:** For a system of charges. For single charge, potential is defined relative to reference at infinity.

---

**Q92:** Force on charge q at distance r from charge Q in medium of dielectric constant K...
**A:** F = (1/4πKε₀) × (qQ/r²)

---

**Q93:** How does a charged comb attract paper pieces?
**A:** Comb polarizes paper (induces dipole), non-uniform field of comb exerts force on dipole, pulling paper toward comb.

---

**Q94:** Why can't a charge be held in stable equilibrium by electrostatic forces alone?
**A:** Earnshaw's theorem states that for point charges, potential energy has no local minima. Equilibrium is unstable.

---

**Q95:** Electric potential V and field E relationship.
**A:** E = -dV/dr (in 1D). Vector: E = -∇V

---

**Q96:** Does Coulomb's law satisfy Newton's third law?
**A:** Yes. F₁₂ = -F₂₁ (equal and opposite).

---

**Q97:** When is Coulomb's law not directly applicable?
**A:** For continuous charge distributions (need integration), or when charges are moving (need relativistic/magnetic corrections).

---

**Q98:** What is the physical significance of dipole moment?
**A:** It quantifies strength and direction of dipole. Larger p → stronger field and torque response.

---

**Q99:** Why is potential constant throughout conductor in electrostatic equilibrium?
**A:** If potential varied, electric field (E = -dV/dr) would be non-zero, causing charge movement. Since equilibrium has no motion, E = 0, hence V is constant.

---

**Q100:** Can electric field exist in vacuum without charges?
**A:** Yes. Electromagnetic waves (including light) propagate electric (and magnetic) fields through vacuum without charges present.
